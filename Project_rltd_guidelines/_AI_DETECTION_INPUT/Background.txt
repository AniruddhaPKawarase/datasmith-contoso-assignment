Most enterprises run on databases that no human reads end-to-end. An Odoo deployment alone exposes about six hundred tables in PostgreSQL covering inventory, purchasing, sales, accounting and manufacturing. Business users want to ask questions in English. The catch is that converting those questions into working SQL fails badly at this scale. The hard numbers are recent and stark. On Spider 2.0, GPT-4o lands at 10.1 percent task success. On BIRD-Ent, with its four-thousand-column schemas, the strongest published methods reach roughly 39.1 percent. A 2024 CIDR position paper from Microsoft (Floratou et al.) argues directly that enterprise NL2SQL is not solved, and points at four reasons: schema complexity, ambiguous wording, semantic drift, and weak benchmarks.

Supply chain is where this breaks loudest. Take an everyday executive question: "compare supplier lead times against defect rates for our Asian vendors in Q1". Answering it pulls fiscal-calendar arithmetic, hierarchy roll-ups across company-region-country-warehouse levels, joins across five to ten tables, and access to procurement plus quality plus partner data at the same time. Industry research on agentic LLMs in supply chains (Klabe et al., Int'l J. Production Research 2025) backs the case that multi-agent systems are the right shape of solution. Walmart, SAP, Microsoft and Google are already piloting agent platforms in this space.

So why do today's multi-agent NL-to-SQL systems still fall short here? Look at MAC-SQL, MARS-SQL, SQL-of-Thought, CHASE-SQL. They all decompose by SQL pipeline stages: schema link, decompose, generate, validate, refine. None of them decompose by business domain. None federate across heterogeneous databases. None ship a temporal-reasoning module specialised for supply chain semantics. And none are evaluated on a realistic supply chain workload. Single-agent prompting collapses on a six-hundred-table schema for a simple reason: relevant evidence is buried, terms like "lead time" or "sales" or "cost" carry several meanings depending on context, and the model has no clean way to ask the user back. That is the gap this dissertation targets.
